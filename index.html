Introducing WEBCHAIN

WEBCHAIN is a new blockchain project that aims to decentralize the internet through a community-owned network. The Webchain blockchain will launch on the fast and scalable Fantom Opera network, allowing it to support a large number of users and transactions.

The goal of Webchain is to shift control and ownership of internet infrastructure away from large corporations and put it back in the hands of individuals and communities. By leveraging blockchain technology and token incentives, Webchain wants to empower users to host their own websites, apps and content in a peer-to-peer fashion without relying on centralized servers or services.

WEBCHAIN will utilize its native WEBC token to fuel this decentralized internet network. 90% of the total WEBC token supply will be distributed to the community through mining and staking rewards. This ensures that the network remains decentralized and controlled by its users rather than a small group of early investors.

In addition to hosting decentralized applications and websites, Webchain aims to build protocols for anonymous payments, private communications, sharing of bandwidth and storage, digital identity and more. All of these functions will be managed through smart contracts without centralized oversight.

By providing users ownership over their data and the ability to participate economically in the growth of the network, Webchain hopes to shift the balance of power away from large tech corporations and towards internet end users. If successful, it could lay the foundation for a truly decentralized alternative to the world wide web as we know it today.

The project is scheduled for launch in Q4 2023 on Fantom Opera. Community members are invited to participate in the testnet starting in June to help test the upcoming protocols and provide feedback to the development team. Webchain aims to build the future of a free, open and community-owned internet.

BITCOIN

Bitcoin is the first decentralized digital currency, known as cryptocurrency. It was created in 2009 by the anonymous identity known as Satoshi Nakamoto and introduced as open-source software. Bitcoin operates without a central authority through a distributed peer-to-peer network, making it impossible to shut down. Transactions are verified by network nodes through cryptography and recorded in a public distributed ledger called the blockchain. Bitcoins can be used to pay for goods and services or traded for traditional currencies like the US dollar on cryptocurrency exchanges.

Some key features that have led to Bitcoin's success include its limited supply capped at 21 million, scarcity driven by mining difficulty, borderless nature with no jurisdiction, censorship resistance, and the implementation of cryptography to secure the network and verify transactions. Using public-key cryptography and digital signatures, Bitcoin allows users to securely send and receive payments without divulging personal information. It introduced the concepts of mining, hashrate, and decentralized consensus that many other cryptocurrencies and blockchain applications have since adopted. By market capitalization, Bitcoin remains the dominant cryptocurrency and has spawned a multi-billion dollar industry.

ETHEREUM

Ethereum is a decentralized, open-source blockchain with smart contract functionality. It was proposed in 2013 by Vitalik Buterin and launched in 2015 via a crowdsale that raised over 31,000 BTC. Ethereum allows developers to build decentralized applications, known as DApps, on top of its blockchain using smart contracts. These smart contracts are pieces of code that run exactly as programmed without any possibility of downtime, censorship, fraud or third party interference.

Some key aspects of Ethereum include its Turing-complete programming language, which allows developers to build a wide variety of decentralized applications that go beyond just currencies and tokens. The Ethereum Virtual Machine (EVM) acts as a global computer for executing smart contracts. Ethereum also introduced the Ether currency (ETH), which helps facilitiate operations on the blockchain and allows users to pay transaction fees. There are many other innovations such as its account-based model with state transitions, native programming language Solidity, and interesting consensus models like proof-of-work and proof-of-stake. Ethereum has grown to become the largest blockchain platform supporting thousands of decentralized apps, financial services, and digital collectibles.

PRIVATEKEYS

What is a Private Key? In cryptocurrency and blockchain systems, a private key is a secret number that allows users to access (send/receive) their funds. It acts as the digital credential for a corresponding cryptocurrency address or public key that holds a balance. Private keys are randomly generated numbers that are stored encrypted in the user's digital wallet.

Some key properties of private keys include:

They are long strings of numbers and letters. Bitcoin private keys are 256 bits while Ethereum uses 256-bit keys derived from the ECDSA curve secp256k1.

Private keys must be kept secret and never shared with anyone. If they are compromised, attackers can access and drain crypto funds from the associated public address.

Private keys allow digital signatures to be created to authorize transactions from a certain address. The wallet software signs transactions with the private key to prove ownership.

They are needed along with the public key/address to spend cryptocurrency funds from a wallet. Without the private key, the corresponding coins cannot be accessed even if the public key is known.

Private keys can generate a new public address or restore access to an existing address it once controlled. This is done through cryptographic key mapping functions.

Writing down or backing up private keys properly is crucial for recovery in case the digital wallet is lost. Proper storage avoids loss of funds from lost keys.

So in summary, a private key grants full access and control over the cryptocurrency funds associated with its paired public key. Private keys must remain secret to keep coins secure.
